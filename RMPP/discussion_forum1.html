Research Methods and Professional Practice - Discussion Forum (Unit 1 - 3)

Malicious input to content filters

August 20, 2024 Wiley Finch

This post will focus on the “Malicious Inputs to Content Filters” (‘Case: Malicious Inputs to Content Filters - ACM Ethics’, 2018). This case study focuses on “Blocker Plus”, a filtering software that censors inappropriate content for minors. Recently Blocker Plus started doing this by utilizing a classification model that based on user input creates a blacklist of inappropriate content online. This design has been compromised by activist groups resulting in false blocks of content regarding sexual orientation, vaccination, and climate change among others. As a response, Blocker Plus has blocked the accounts of the malicious input hoping the model would correct itself over time.

The analysis of the case study focuses on multiple breaches of the ACM code of Ethics. Firstly, the use of AI and the classification model results in violations of principles 2.5, 2.8, and 2.9 (‘Case: Malicious Inputs to Content Filters - ACM Ethics’, 2018). The main problem with AI technology is that the current boom of technology is that there is no real reinforcement of ethical handling, resulting in organizations solely focusing on the economic benefits of employing these systems (Hagendorff, 2020). However, given the fact that this is an IT system, at least fundamentals like older computer-based legalization and ethics should still apply.

Furthermore, the analysis focuses on discriminating based on sexual orientation, violating Principles 1.2 and 1.4. This also violates the BCS Code of Conduct that IT should be made for everyone (BCS Code of Conduct for members - Ethics for IT professionals | BCS, no date).

Lastly, the response of Blocker Plus is analyzed. Hoping that the system would correct itself is a violation of Principles 1.3, 2.4, and 2.7. This also violates the BCS Code of Conduct “Show what you know, learn what you don’t” points 5 and 6 (BCS Code of Conduct for members - Ethics for IT professionals | BCS, no date).

On a final note, it is noteworthy that the U.S. CIPA is named as the compliant legal framework. Recently the Online Safety Act has been employed by the U.K. government, placing responsibility for censoring content for minors on the owner of the content (Online Safety Act: explainer, 2024). Seeing the internet as a global space, more and more online entities will need to be compliant with this legalization, hopefully resulting in a more cyber-secure environment for children.
