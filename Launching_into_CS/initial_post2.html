Launching into CS - Initial Post (Unit 6)

Identification and discussion of two security technologies and the context in which they can be employed.

September 14, 2023 Wiley Finch

One of the most visible cyber-attacks for end users is the Denial of Service (DoS) attack. The attack targets the availability of the victim’s resources resulting in frustrated users, lost business, and a damaged reputation. The Distributed DoS (DDoS) attack has a growing number of reported incidents, making it one of the most important and fatal threats (Mansfield-Devine, 2015). A DDoS attack generates flooding traffic to a specific resource to obstruct the legitimate flow of information. This post will identify and discuss two security technologies to tackle DDoS attacks and how they can be employed within an organization.

One possible technology to protect against a DDoS attack is utilizing a Demilitarized zone (DMZ) in the network design. A DMZ is a carefully controlled perimeter network that is used as a barrier between public or non-trusted networks and internal or protected networks (Rogers, 2022). The performance of DMZ is mostly effective in combination with one or two firewalls (Rababah, Zhou and Bader, 2018). The research of Patel (2020) shows that a network designed with one firewall (Cisco ASA) provided enhanced security and performance against TCP SYN flood and ICMP flood attacks. Designing and employing this technology however can be challenging, especially in virtual environments, such as complexity, and risk of misconfiguration (Kasapovic and Biberkic, 2015). Regardless of these disadvantages, investing in the correct employment and maintenance of this technology offers a better defence against DDoS attacks.

One other technology to tackle DDoS attacks is to validate legitimate users via a challenge-response authentication, the best-known example being CAPTCHA. The enormous growth in computational power and artificial intelligence over the last few years makes it so that computers can easily tackle challenge-response authentication. One prime example can be found in the event ticketing industry, proving the weakness of CAPTCHAs against ticket robots (E. Kaiser and W. -c. Feng, 2010). Another approach to challenge-response authentication is proof-of-work systems, that require more resources from a user than CAPTCHA. Although using proof-of-work systems can provide higher security, it is proven to be uneconomical and negatively affects usability (Laurie and Clayton, 2004). This leaves the flawed CAPTCHA to be the favourable choice of protection. However, new research such as Patil (2023) might provide a more applicable proof-of-work puzzle system for authentication.
